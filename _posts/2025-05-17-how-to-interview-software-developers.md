"You need to do a better job of finding similarities with candidates despite having different backgrounds," I heard as constructive feedback from our Human Resources recuriter.

"Engineering interviews are just like Sales interviews! You guys are always looking to eliminate and trick people, and to measure how much you know versus potential!" my PM said to me during our one-on-one meeting.

Of course, neither of them had actually been present during the technical portions of the interview process, but their insticts were correct, we were doing a poor job of evaluating candidates based on potential. We were elminiating strong-but-nervous candidates who fumbled simple things, and advancing candidates who demonstrated confidence and strong technical skills but who ended up not being capable of doing the job. Out of 10 candidates, none had made it out of the technical round of the process. Was the process broken, or were we doing a poor job advancing candidates? Maybe we were not able to attract the talent we were looking for? Why was interviewign broken, and what could we do to fix it?

In my experience, most interviews for development positions are conducted in ways that will lead to poor outcomes for both the employeer and candidates. We can do better.

# What are the goals of a technical interview round?

It surprised me to learn that my father-in-law was an Electrical Engineer but had never attended college. He was a Vietnam vet who moved to Texas and worked for Texas Instruments. Obviously he was a smart and capable person, but I assumed that even then, a college degree would be necessary for working in that field. How is it that a generation later, it's unheard of for a non-Engineer grad to have an Engineering role? I don't mean Software Engineering, which could mean so many things to so many people, but Electrical and Mechanical Engineering? My father-in-law achieved his Engineering position by working hard, demonstrating potential and aptitude, and received on-the-job training. Why don't we do this anymore?

My own software development career started with unofficially writing scripts to automate parts of my Technical Writing job. I had graduated with an English degree with a Technical Writing concentration, but the job I was able to get after college was a lot more copy-and-paste-and-reformat than actual writing. It was extremely boring, but I was good at it, got my work done quickly and flawlessly, and that gave me time to think about how what I did could be improved and automated. I was making $24k a year in 2006 and slowly started teaching myself enough advanced Excel, RegExp, VisualBasic, VBA, Microsoft Access and, eventually, Ruby, to start writing more complicated programs for myself and others on evenings and weekends.

# How do you achieve these goals?

There seem to be several schools of thought around how to conduct technical interviews for software development:

- Before interviewing, decide what skills you need the candidates to be able to demonstrate. Work with your PM and HR (if you have them) to ensure that your technical interview round focuses on the skills that the job requires.
- For example, a job may require candidates to write software in a specific flavor of React and Redux. Should you select for candidates that claim to already know React and Redux? Should you select only for candidates who claim to already know React? Do you need someone who can simply "hit the ground running?" What does that even mean in the age of LLM-powered code assistants that can translate prose into code?
 
- Having candidates demonstrating core Computer Science competency by asking them to whiteboard algorithms.
- Asking candidates to write code live, possibly as a pairing session with a senior developer.
- Giving candidates a "take-home" exercise that lets them write and submit code on their own time.

# What are some practices to avoid? 

Keep code pairing sessions short.

Avoid gotcha-style questions.

Don't have vague evaluation criteral for .

If you're going to ask candidates to submit a code sample or write code for a take home exercise, make sure that you follow up with them about the results of that. This doesn't need to be  a full code review, but there should at least be a brief discussion about the submission. Avoid rejecting candidates if their coding practices don't exactly match yours, unless you explicitl  said specifically that they should write code in a specific way.

Don't leave your candidates hanging when it comes to feedback.
